What whole life insurance is (and isn’t)

Whole life insurance is a form of permanent life insurance: when premiums are paid as required, the death benefit lasts your lifetime, subject to the insurer keeping the promises spelled out in your contract. Many policies build cash value over time with guarantees and non-guaranteed elements—details vary by carrier and product. Nothing here is tax or legal advice; we review illustrations and documents with you line by line.

Why families ask about whole life

People often consider whole life when they want lifetime protection, predictable premiums, or a structured way to accumulate policy cash value while keeping protection in place. We compare those goals with alternatives—such as term insurance paired with other savings—so you choose with context, not pressure.

Whole life insurance FAQs

What is whole life insurance?

Permanent coverage with level premiums that may build cash value as defined in your policy—lifetime protection when you keep the contract in good standing.

How is whole life different from term life insurance?

Term covers a fixed period at a typically lower upfront cost; whole life emphasizes lifetime coverage and contract-defined cash-value mechanics.
